General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

The general data protection regulation is a regulation in the European Union law on data protection and privacy for all individuals within the EU and the European Economic Area.

It came into action on 25th May 2018.

It specifies how businesses and public sector organisations can handle the information of their customers.

It gives more rights and control over their information.

It applies to Personal data:

  • Any information relating to an identifiable person who can be directly or indirectly identified in particular by reference to an identifier.

  • Applies to both automated personal data and to manual filing systems where personal data are accessible according to specific criteria.

The GDPR applies to processing carried out by organisations operating within the EU, or outside the EU offering goods or services to individuals in the EU.

The GDPR does not apply to certain activities including processing covered by the Law Enforcement Directive, processing for national security purposes, or processing carried out by individuals purely for personal/household activities.

The GDPR applies to both ‘controllers’ and ‘processors’.

A controller determintes the purposes and means of processing personal data, and has obligations to ensure their contracts with processors comply with the GDPR.

A processor is responsible for processing personal data on behanf of a controller. They are required to maintain records of personal data and processing activities, and have legal liability if they are responsible for a breach.

Lawful bases for processing

Consent

The individual must have given clear consent for you to process their personal data for a specific purpose

Contract

The processing must be necessary for a contract you have with the individual, or because they have asked you to take specific steps before entering into a contract.

Legal obligation

The processing is necessary for you to comply with the law (not including contractual obligations).

Individual Rights (GDPR)

The GDPR provides the following rights for individuals:

  • The right to be informed

  • The right of access

  • The right to rectification

  • The right to erasure

  • The right to restrict processing

  • The right to data portability

  • The right to object

  • Rights in relation to automated decision making and profiling.

The Data Protection Act (2018)

The data protection act of 2018 can be summarised into 6 key principles.

  1. processing of data must be lawful, fair and transparent
  2. the purposes of processing must be specified, explicit and legitimate
  3. stored personal data must be adequate, relevant and not excessive
  4. stored personal data must be accurate and kept up to date
  5. stored personal data must be kept for no longer than is necessary
  6. stored personal data must be processed in a secure manner

Intellectual Property (IP)

Intellectual property is intangible property (knowledge) created by an individual or organisation.

Things like literary works, music, video, art, designs, inventions and trademarks are all examples of intellectual property.

Often, a large amount of time, effort and funding is involved in the development of IP, and the producer needs protection against other individuals or corporations profiting from their work without consent.

There are two main types of protection:

Copyright

Comes into effect automatically - protects the expression or manifestation of an idea, but not the idea itself.

Copyright covers more traditional documents like books, music articles, and movies as well as things like software, databases, designs and other types of multimedia.

It normally extends beyond the author’s lifetime, but there are some exceptions:

  • Sound recordings and broadcasts - 50 years

  • Published editions (typographical arrangement) - 25 years

Authors may licence others to produce copies, often in return for royalties

Copyright can be transferred

While software is automatically copyrighted, this doesn’t prevent others from writing theor own code to implement the same ideas.

Patent

A more powerful means of protecting IP.

Protects the idea behind an invention, not just the manifestation of it

The ideas should be original and not obvious

Patents aren’t automatic - they need to be applied for

Patents are published, thus benefiting public knowledge

There are clear boundaries defining what can be patented. Software is not usually patentable, and artistic creations, mathematical models, plans, schemes or other purely mental processes also can’t be patented.

Licence categories

Commercial (Perpetual Use)

One-off fee, and usually only one machine

Commercial (Annual Fee)

A subscription model, where an access key is provided and updated annually.

Shareware

Free trial period Purchase required after to avoid infringing copyright

Freeware

Compiled vs open-source still subject to terms and conditions, so copyright applies may only be free for certain categories of use (e.g. non-profit, academic)
